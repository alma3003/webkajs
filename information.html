Mythology

Peri

The image of the nymph - peri (among other peoples of the Pari) — was preserved by the Tajik, Pamir peoples, Afghans, Persians, Baluchs, and then strengthened in the Turkic-speaking mythology. Benevolent bets appear in mythology in the form of a bird, a beaver or a non-poisonous white or yellow snake, and evil ones appear in the form of poisonous snakes, frogs, turtles, predatory animals, for example, a tiger. Peris are endowed with different qualities. If in many Turkic-speaking and Iranian-speaking peoples they are perceived as evil spirits, then among the Kazakhs these creatures are considered representatives of a special people. The Peris are divided into Muslim and Kapirs. There are also water peris - Su peris. They can also be considered beautiful aerial creatures that live between heaven and earth in the mountains of Kaf. Peri girls are very beautiful, young men often fall in love with them. In Kazakh mythology, mermaids are considered one of the types of su peri - water fairies. Usually they tickle their victims to death and only then drag them to the bottom.

Zhalmauyz kempir

Sometimes this witch is represented with seven heads, long sharp nails on her hands and feet and a huge hump, because of which she bends almost to the ground. Many consider her a terrible creature that drinks human blood. There is no exact information about her gender. This creature feeds only on humans. Most often, it rubs into the confidence of young girls and quietly drinks their blood. Also, Zhalmauyz can take the form of an object, swim up to a person unnoticed and tightly cling to him.

Zheztyrnak

Usually, the harlot represents a beautiful and beautiful girl who hides her hands under her arms. She wears a lot of gold and silver decorations. A special betrayal is represented by her brass nails: they pop into the throat and can cut all the blood in a person. The main attributes are a cage with gold coins, a silver ribbon with decoration, which is inserted into the braid. According to legends, in caves, where they live, there are many reservoirs. The image of zheztyrnaka meets in the myths of other peoples. In Kyrgyz mythology, these are "zheztumshuk", "shulbus", under the name of which Altay-sayansky tyurkov is also met in folklore. Zheztyrnak takes part in stories, epics and legends "Kulamergen", "clay batyr from the Earth", "alaman and Zholaman", "Alpamys batyr", "Otegen batyr".

Albasty

In Kazakh mythology, Albasty is represented as one of the images of a pest, an evil demon. He is often associated with an aquatic creature, although Albasty can transform into any creature. This is a fat, hairy woman with sharp fangs sticking out of her mouth, whose sagging chest reaches the ground. Albasty is found in the myths of all Turkic-speaking peoples, but the representation and images vary in different countries. Kazakh albasts are divided into two types. They come in black and yellow. In Tuvan mythology, these creatures have one eye and a nose made of stone. In some myths, it is characterized as a creature with an open back and protruding internal organs. They harm young women and children, come in nightmares.

Mythology

Sorel

Shokan Valikhanov in his writings mentioned a demonological being - sorel. Living in the woods, he kills a man by tickling him to death. Also from the writings it is known about another evil being - konayak. This evil demon has a human torso and a long tail instead of legs. He lived on islands, in forests and by roads. Meeting a person, konayak attacked him, sat on horseback, twining his tail, and rode. The weakened victim could be strangled and eaten.

Aidahar

This is a dragon, which in Kazakh legends appears in the form of a huge snake. The word aidahar comes from the ancient Iranian-Avestan mythical character Dahak. The image of the modern folk dragon is fully formed under the influence of medieval Arab-Persian fairy-epic traditions, that is, it is formed in accordance with their character. They are depicted as one-, three-, seven-headed monsters living in caves and wells. According to the most common plot, he steals a girl, hides her, and then eats her. Among the Saks in 4-3 thousand BC, aidahar was perceived as the progenitor, the mother of mankind. In mythology, aidahar personifies the zoomorphic nature of the three worlds — serpentine, underground and aquatic. The book "Mythology of the Predkazakhs" describes the place and meaning of snakes in Kazakh mythology. The positive cult of the snake was present even in the mythology of the Turks. The Turkic peoples perceived the snake as the keeper of the hearth. The snake that got into the house could not be killed — it was necessary to pour koumiss on her head, thereby escorting her outside. The perception of Aidahar as the personification of evil came with the advent of Islam on the territory of Kazakhstan.
